IN THE HIGH COURT OF JUSTICE — KING'S BENCH DIVISION
Claimant's Skeleton Argument — TEST DOCUMENT 01 (control: all clean)

1. The claimant advances its claim in negligence and in contract.

2. Duty of care. The modern test for the existence of a duty of care is the
three-stage test in Caparo Industries plc v Dickman [1990] 2 AC 605:
foreseeability, proximity, and whether it is fair, just and reasonable to impose
a duty. That analysis builds on the neighbour principle in Donoghue v Stevenson
[1932] AC 562.

3. Negligent misstatement. Where the loss is purely economic and flows from
reliance on a statement, liability is governed by Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller
& Partners Ltd [1964] AC 465, which turns on an assumption of responsibility.

4. Remoteness of damage. Losses are recoverable only within the two limbs of
Hadley v Baxendale (1854) 9 Ex 341 — loss arising naturally, or loss within the
reasonable contemplation of both parties at the time of contracting.

5. Inducing breach of contract. The defendant's procurement of the third party's
breach engages the tort first recognised in Lumley v Gye (1853) 2 E&B 216.

6. For these reasons the claimant invites the court to find in its favour.
